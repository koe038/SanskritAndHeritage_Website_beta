Introduction

Natural Language Processing is regarded as the hardest branch of Artificial Intelligence. This can be attributed the fact that English(most popular language in the world) has a lot of ambiguity which leads to noisy data collection and thus poor accuracy at test time. A lot of NLP research in Sanskrit has shown better results than English for the same architecture. Based on the results, Scientists have put forth some arguments explaining why.

    Phonetics: In English the sounds of the alphabets(26) clash with their counterparts in quite a few occasions. While Sanskrit makes use of 16 Swar and 33 Vyanjan hence clashing is significantly reduced. Example, when you open a mouth and take a sound from the glottis, the word sound has (अ) there, whereas when the mouth opens up wider it is an (आ) sound. English or any other language in comparison has only an (A) equivalent to ए. Another consequence of number of alphabets is evident as the range of English vocabulary also becomes lesser because the phonetics of a lot of alphabets do not map to individual phonetics of the Sanskrit Varnas.
    Structure of the language: Each word in Sanskrit can be divided into sub words and this process would continue and the division would stop when we reach a Dhatu. This is similar to algorithmic structure. Ambiguity arises while combining or dividing words could be eliminated by Fuzzy logic or Fuzzy reasoning techniques.
    Words: Sanskrit dictionary is a very rich one hence representing a word at the machine level is relatively easy. Consider, “I have glasses. “. The stress on this sentence is on the object which is “glasses”. The most obvious question here is what glasses are we talking about, pair of glasses used as a pair of lenses to correct vision or the glass tumblers …. ? In Sanskrit, these two words by their construction are different.



What we did?

    We classified all Sanskrit Slokas into three categories: Chanakya Slokas, Vidur Niti Slokas and all the others as a generic Sanskrit-Slogan category.
    Our aim was to classify a given unseen Sloka correctly to one of these categories.
    We pre-processed the data and used the classical LSTM(Long Short-Term Memory) architecture for the task of classification.
    In 1997, LSTM was proposed by Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber. By introducing Constant Error Carousel (CEC) units, LSTM deals with the vanishing gradient problem. The initial version of LSTM block included cells, input and output gates
    LSTM networks were a major component of a network that achieved a record 17.7% phoneme error rate on the classic TIMIT natural speech dataset in 2013.
    Google started using an LSTM for speech recognition on Google Voice in 2015. Now the optimizations had cut down the errors by about 49%.
    Facebook performed some 4.5 billion automatic translations every day using long short-term memory networks in 2017.

Some Interesting Aspects

    The first computational problem attacked in the framework of the Sanskrit Heritage platform wassegmentation, i.e. sandhi-viccheda. Sandhi occurs in Sanskrit in several places. In generativemorphology, it occurs internally for stem formation and affixes glueing, for instance for declensionand conjugation. This so-called internal sandhi is complex, since it gives rise to long-distanceretroflexion, not easily invertible by finite-state methods.
    Recently, the news speculating around many prestigious tech-science circles is that NASA has made an incredible discovery about computing language for Artificial intelligence. According to research, NASA claims that Sanskrit – the ancient Hindu language – is the most suitable language to develop computer programming for their Artificial Intelligence program. The grammar of Sanskrit is rule-bound, formula-bound, and logical, which makes it highly appropriate to write algorithms.
    In the natural language models like LSTM using recurrent neural networks, there is an under lying assumption that next word is dependent on the word preceeding it, but in Sanskrit order of the word doesn't generally play an important role in language understanding. This possess another challenge in sanskrit with NLP. To sort out this problem, some state of art algorithms have used a graph based approach to find dependency between each word in a sentence and then parse it to obtain relevant information.
